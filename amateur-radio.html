Handbook for Amateur Radio

This handbook is useful for quickly referencing radio-related material.  It covers the character set of International Morse Code, procedural signals, the R-S-T system, acronyms and abbreviations, Q-codes, and the International Phonetic Alphabet, modern and archaic.  Available for download in Portable Document Format.

44, dear reader!  I am Moses K. Frost, SMØ-863Ø.

 My interest in this activity lies in Morse Code, which I have been mastering since MMXVII.  I initially found S. Morse's code system thanks to my tendency to find interesting languages to learn.  (Of course, I had heard of Morse Code previously, but it never caught my mind until then.)  I thought the concept of communicating with only on and off signals was really fascinating.  After three days of studying, I had already learned most of the character set.

 One day, I found <morsecode.me>.  That was the first time I had put my knowledge to use, and communicated with other people in code on my spacebar—simulating a key.  After a few years, I found their Reddit forum, reached out to B. Kanber—the developer of the website—and told him how much I appreciated his project.  About a year after that, he had established a Discord server.  I recall seeing a link to it on the website, when I joined the community, presto.

 There, I met Jason, K6JSC.  Not only did he shortly become a great friend of mine, but likewise an Elmer,¹ who has taught me a lot about Amateur Radio.  He has greatly helped me throughout my journey, and I would not have made it here without him.  Give him the gold ingot, please.

 Jason introduced me to V-Band : a fantastic on-line Morse chat room.  Try it out for Yourself here!²  That day, we established my first simulated experience of Amateur Radio, with a QSO³ that lasted four hours  from two to six of the clock!  I have since made many more contacts on there, and it has truly supported me in developing my skills.  A great thanks to David and Byon, who are the developers of this neat tool.  Their platform, Ham Radio Solutions, also has a Discord server, through which I met Erik, K5EDH.

 Jason introduced me also to Vail : a cool alternative to V-Band.  Try it out for Yourself here!²  I have had much fun there, and I can highly recommend it to the proficient Brass Pounders out there.  Vail, too, has a Discord server, and should You experience any difficulties with either site, I advise You join the communities and start troubleshooting—or just to chat!

For the Christmas of MMXXI, I wanted an iambic Paddle.  I did my research, and found Kari, SMØHRP : the sole proprietor of DX Supply.  I arranged with him an order for my Vibrocube Standard Upgraded.  At that time, I was trying to seek an Amateur Radio club, so while on the phone line with him, I took the opportunity to inquire if he knew of any ; indeed did he !  He suggested SKØQO, and forwarded me to Lasse, SMØFDO.

 After the line was cut, I dialed the number to which he referred me.  I introduced myself and stated my intentions, after which we spoke for a long while.  We arranged a meeting on the eighteenth of December : a Youngsters on the Air event, that was to be hosted at the QTH⁴ of the club.  There, I met Lasse for the first time—without whom I would not be here, either—along with Kalle, SMØNUE, and Robban, SMØTAE.

 I want to shout out the following persons, as well, which I met later on :  Micke (SMØLYC), Uffe (SMØDWK), Hans (SM5JDL, from whom I got my radio), and Linus (SM5OUU).

On the eighteenth of June, MMXXII, I was introduced to Larry, K7HN, on the YouTube channel of Ham Radio Live.  He is live mostly every day, with updates on the latest Amateur Radio news.  I greatly recommend You check him out, and become a part of the show Yourself!

Keys

I have one handpump from the Swedish Navy, that I got from SKØQO.  I have increased its tension by adding an additional spring, and adjusted a larger gap ; more tactile feedback, which I find satisfying—all of which do make the Vibrocube more comfortable for rag chewing.  I have made a few modifications to this key, including a connection re-rout to two contact posts, and a mirror-finish polish.

 At the HamFest in Eskilstuna, on the fifteenth of April, MMXXIII, I got another Swedish Navy handpump in a different model.  It looks older than the first, albeit I unfortunately lack access to documentation.  (A picture will be uploaded here soon.)

 On the aforementioned on-line chat rooms, however, I mostly operate my Vibrocube—that thing weighs three kilograms !  Essentially why I bought it in the first place.  It is very nice to use, as there are jeweled movements in the fulcra of each paddle.  I can send comfortably at thirty-five words per minute, when connected to my Daiwa DK-210.

Bugs

I believe this deserves a separate segment.  I mainly operate MacElroy's Hole-in-the-Wall, which I am borrowing from Lasse.  I practice operating this Bug every day, on just my radio's sidetone, or in V-Band and Vail ; many think I have become quite efficient with it.

 Buck, AA4SN, is a skilled manufacturer of his very own Bugs, a Home-Brewed Vertical Bug of which he and his club were so kind to gift me!  I am truly grateful, and should You read this, Buck & company, I want to heartfeltly thank You.
